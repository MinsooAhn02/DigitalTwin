Hello everyone.
For this mid-check, we will show what our system can do now, what problems remain, and what
we plan to improve next.

We load CCTV locations for the current map area via the ITS OpenAPI and place markers on
the map. Clicking a marker opens a floating panel that plays the live HLS stream using HLS.js.

The panel has two tabs — live video and YOLO detection. On the YOLO tab, the browser
captures frames from the video, sends them to the backend via WebSocket, and displays the
annotated result. We took this approach because the ITS HLS URL contains an expiring token,
making direct OpenCV access on the backend unreliable.

In the detection pipeline, YOLOv8 identifies cars, buses, trucks, and motorcycles, and
ByteTrack maintains consistent IDs across frames. Results are reflected on the map and sidebar.

We also fixed a frame-ordering issue where delayed frames could appear after newer ones.
The backend now drops stale frames so the visualization stays current.

For performance, the target was to match the 15–30 fps of the ITS stream, which means the
full pipeline — canvas capture, WebSocket round trip, inference, and re-encode — must finish
within 33ms. Fixed overhead accounts for roughly 17ms, leaving 16ms for inference. After
benchmarking multiple model and input size combinations with a TensorRT FP16 engine, we
chose yolov8s + imgsz=640: 3ms inference, ~20ms total, ~50fps throughput. Full resolution
is preserved to minimize accuracy loss, and for large objects like vehicles yolov8s is
sufficient.

---

Now, what still needs improvement.

The first issue is measurement accuracy. Speed is currently calculated using a fixed frame rate assumption, but the actual detection interval varies at runtime, so the values are not precise.

The second issue is camera angle. Public CCTV cameras are mounted at various fixed angles, and when the angle is poor, our pixel-to-GPS mapping becomes unreliable — which also makes speed and position estimates less accurate.

The third issue is detection scope. Since we are using street-facing CCTV, the field of view often includes parking lots, sidewalks, or side streets, so parked or off-road vehicles can be picked up as moving traffic and add noise to the data.

These are the main limitations we are aware of at this stage.
